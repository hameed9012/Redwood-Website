# Redwood Peak — Discord Bot · Brainstorm v2 (focused)

**Reframe (corrected):** the roleplay happens in **ERLC**. The bot and the website are the *records-and-utility layer* wrapped around it — not a game. The org runs top-down on what **High Command** says. The river-poisoning was one RP, not the identity of the company. So this bot's job is: keep meticulous, slightly-menacing company records, handle the org's paperwork/identity/supply, secure the Discord, and keep the website as a live mirror of what the org actually does. Every feature is a *tool* or *atmosphere*, never a minigame.

**Voice:** the bot IS Redwood Peak — deadpan, over-polite, faintly threatening. That personality runs through everything.

10 ideas, written out.

---

### 1. The Shift System (the "ankle monitor")
When you go on duty as Redwood in ERLC, you `/shift start`; the bot logs your on-duty time, and it's mandatory — no unlogged shifts. When you `/shift end`, it walks you through the close-out: first, **account for your movements** — the landmarks in the county you passed through — which is the ankle-monitor conceit: the company always knows where its people were, or where they *said* they were. Then it prompts two records: a **report** in the witness-dossier style if you had a notable encounter (which publishes to the website's Witness Dossiers), and an **incident log** capturing everything major from the shift — stops, deliveries, interactions, problems. All of it lands on the site as a permanent, timestamped entry tied to your identity, so there's always a paper trail and High Command can see the whole org's activity at a glance. This is a records concept, not a nagging timer — no random "prove you're here" pings. Just a company that writes everything down and files it. *"Shift closed. Account for your movements."*

### 2. ⭐ Identity Packets
When someone new joins, High Command runs `/identity create` on them. The **first time**, it does two things at once: it generates a complete cover identity — a randomly-generated legal name, DOB, a clearly-fake ID/SSN (fictional format), blood type, next-of-kin, and the rest of the paperwork, rendered in the site's document style — **and** it onboards them for real: assigns their Redwood employee role(s) and sets their server nickname to their new **employee name**. Getting your identity and getting hired are the same motion. If `/identity create` is run again on the **same Discord account**, it keeps that established employee name (no re-onboarding) but **rotates everything else** — new legal name, new numbers, fresh paperwork — because sometimes a person needs a new identity while staying the same employee. (No avatar-stamping — just the generated data.) It makes "getting your identity" a real ritual and "needing a new one" a real event.

### 3. ⭐ Serial Number Generator
`/serial` asks what kind of firearm it is and mints a registration-style serial number for it — formatted like the real thing but obviously fictional. Pure roleplay lubricant for ERLC: when a gun changes hands, gets used, or gets recovered, it now *has a number*, and a number can be looked up. The bot keeps a lightweight registry — which serial belongs to which identity, whether it's clean or flagged, when it was issued — so a weapon carries a history. That's where the flavor is: High Command pulls a serial and it traces back to a burned identity, or comes up "never registered," and that fact is itself a story. Small command, big intrigue, and it slots right into the identity/paperwork spine.

### 4. ⭐ Identity Burn & Re-issue
Every cover has a lifespan. As an identity gathers attention — named in an incident, flagged by High Command, "known to law enforcement" after an ERLC scene — it can be marked compromised. **Burning** it retires that cover: the clean record, the commendations, the standing tied to that name are gone, and the person continues under a fresh packet (the re-roll from #2). This gives the identity system real weight — a spotless identity is worth protecting, and torching one to shake heat is a genuine sacrifice. It's also a High Command lever: they can *order* an identity burned. On the website, a burned identity gets archived/redacted in Personnel.

### 5. Contraband & Cash Inventory
A set of company books for what the org actually holds — **medical supplies / product** on one side, **cash** on the other — each split into **white** (clean, on the books) and **black** (off the books). Employees fulfilling orders draw stock down; High Command tops it up; the numbers move as the org operates. The clean/dirty split is the whole point: the legitimate-front-vs-real-business tension made legible, feeding the website ledger so the public books can be shown to look spotless while the real inventory lives behind clearance. Not a game — just an accurate, atmospheric set of books High Command genuinely manages.

### 6. Orders → Ledger (with invoices)
The order pipeline, built out. An order enters from the website's Contact page **or** a Discord embed that opens a DM with the bot ("What are you looking to order?" → product, quantity, drop, status). It lands as an open order employees can see; someone claims it, supplies it in ERLC, and marks it done — at which point the earnings post to the **website ledger** as an explainable inflow, so the money always has a paper story if anyone investigates. Folded in: the bot can generate a shareable **invoice / pay page** for an order — a clean, legit-looking bill — that reconciles against the ledger automatically. This is the org's actual revenue engine and the strongest reason the bot and site have to talk to each other.

### 7. ⭐ Two-way Live Site Sync
The seam runs both directions. Bot actions **write to the live website** — dossiers from shift reports, inflows to the ledger, identities to Personnel, directives to the Command page. Website events **ping Discord** — a new Contact-form order opens an order thread automatically; a published dossier announces in a channel with its redactions intact. The result: the website stops being a static brochure and becomes a live operational record of the server that nobody has to hand-edit. This is what makes the whole Redwood ecosystem feel real — what you do in the org shows up on the company's site.

### 8. ⭐ The Security Suite
The full defensive layer, themed as site security. Anti-raid / spam / nuke protection as **perimeter security** ("Unregistered personnel detected. Lockdown initiated."). **Alt / intruder detection** framed as catching a spy (flag/alert only — no verification gate; Roblox↔Discord verification is handled by an external bot like Bloxlink, so ours never does member onboarding). A `/lockdown` that seals the server for incidents ("the site is sealed"). **Clearance-gated channels** mapped to the website tiers, so your standing in the org gates what you can see. And a **dead-man's switch** — a hidden command that, if High Command doesn't periodically reset it, fires a "purge" *alert* (echoing the site's Purge button that only ever asks you to reconsider — alert only, nothing destructive). It's a genuinely competent security bot; the theming just makes every alert read as part of the story instead of a mod-log line.

### 9. Directives & Tasking (High Command → the org)
Since the org runs on what High Command says, give that a spine. High Command issues **directives/taskings** through the bot — to a person, a rank, or everyone — with a body, a priority, and an optional deadline. Recipients get pinged, acknowledge, and mark complete; the bot tracks who's still outstanding. Standing directives (the cold, numbered rules) and one-off taskings both live here and publish to the website's **Command** page, giving the chain of command a visible, permanent record. It turns "High Command told us to do X" from a message that scrolls away into a tracked, accountable order — exactly how a records-obsessed org would actually run.

### 10. ⭐ The Bot's Persona + the Employee Handbook
The bot IS the company. Every confirmation, error, and help text is in-voice — deadpan, over-polite, faintly threatening ("Regrettably, that action is above your clearance." / "Your movements have been recorded. Thank you for your cooperation."). `/help` isn't a command list, it's the **Employee Handbook** — the code of conduct that reads normal at the top and gets quietly unsettling toward the end. This costs almost nothing and is the single biggest reason the bot won't feel like another Carl/Dyno clone: it has a personality, and that personality is the company watching you.

# Second pass — 10 more (for approval)
New ideas, no overlap with the approved set above. Same rules: tools/atmosphere, not a game; ERLC-anchored; the records-obsessed company.

### 11. Unified Records Lookup — `/lookup` (the "police database")
The company's memory, made queryable — and it's fed by the org's own paperwork. Every incident and interaction log (from the shift close-out, #1) **requires** structured fields naming *everyone involved*: names, cover names, car plates, and for law-enforcement contacts the officer's name and badge number, unit, location. Log a speeding stop and you record the officer, their badge, the plate, the spot — and from then on that officer, that badge, that plate is in the system. Over enough shifts the org quietly assembles its own **"police database"**: who patrols where, which badges keep showing up, which plates are trouble. `/lookup` is how you read it — feed it a member, a cover name, a serial, a plate, a name, or a badge and it returns the consolidated file: the employee behind it, current and past identities, standing, clearance, flags, the incidents they appear in, the weapons and vehicles registered to them, and — for outsiders logged in incidents — everything the org has ever written down about them. It's the connective tissue for the whole system, so nothing anyone logged is ever really gone. Clearance-gated: a Recruit sees the surface, High Command sees the redactions too.

### 12. Personnel Roster & Rank Management
Identity packets create the person; this manages their place in the org. High Command hires, promotes, demotes, transfers, and dismisses through the bot, and the whole chain of command lives as a managed roster that mirrors to the website's Personnel page in real time. Ranks map to clearance (and therefore to gated channels and to what `/lookup` reveals), so a promotion is a real event with consequences across Discord and the site. Dismissal isn't just removing a role — it runs the paperwork: the identity archived, the standing closed out, the file stamped. It's the org's actual HR spine, and because it's the source of truth for rank, everything else (directives, requisitions, IA) can trust it.

### 13. Vehicle & Plate Registry
The serial generator's twin, for ERLC's vehicles. Register a vehicle and its plate to an identity — clean or flagged, with an issue date and a history — so that when a car turns up at a scene, someone abandons one, or a plate gets called in, there's a record to pull. A recovered vehicle traces back to a burned identity; a plate comes up "not on file," which is its own red flag. It slots into the same registry logic as weapons and into `/lookup`, so an identity accumulates a footprint — the guns they've held, the cars they've driven — and a footprint is exactly what a paranoid company keeps.

### 14. Operational Budgets & Shift-End Reconciliation
Not a store — an operating allowance. Every week, High Command issues a **budget** to each member (or by rank), and it **stacks**: the new grant adds onto whatever was left, so everyone carries a running balance with a clear total-granted vs total-spent. Members spend from that balance on whatever an operation actually needs — freely, in the moment. The reconciliation happens at **shift's end**, folded into the close-out: you itemize what you bought and what each thing cost, and the bot deducts every line from your balance. It's the economy-bot idea done properly for an org: weekly top-ups, carryover, self-reported spend, and a per-member balance High Command can see at a glance — and question, if your spend doesn't line up with your shift or your movements. Overspend and you're in the red until next week's grant; underspend and it banks. (Replaces the old "requisition from inventory" framing — cleaner, and it makes every shift close-out also a little expense report.)

### 15. The Document Generator & Vault
Identity packets are one kind of paperwork; this is the whole apparatus. A generator that produces the org's formal documents — NDAs, employment contracts, permits, cease-and-desists, official letters, manifests — in the site's classified-document style, filled from a short prompt, ready to post or file. Everything generated lands in a **vault**: clearance-gated, searchable, so the company's paper trail is real and retrievable. It gives every scene a prop — a contract to sign, a permit to wave, a letter to deliver — and because the documents share the website's Document look, a thing generated in Discord can be published to the site and read as canon.

### 16. Briefings & Muster
Structure for a top-down org. High Command schedules a **briefing** or an operation; the bot announces it, reminds attendees, and at start-time runs a **muster** — a roll call where employees check in as they go on duty in ERLC — producing a clean attendance record of who showed and who didn't. Attendance is itself a record the company keeps (and a thing you can be written up for missing). It turns "we're running an op tonight" from a message into an organized, logged event, and it gives High Command a real handle on turnout without counting reactions by hand.

### 17. Internal Affairs / Loyalty Review
When High Command needs to look hard at one of their own — a suspected leak, a name that keeps appearing in the wrong incidents — they open an **IA review**, and the bot compiles that member's entire file into a single dossier: every identity they've held, the incidents they're named in, their movement trails, their requisitions, any security flags. No dice, no minigame — it's the company turning its own surveillance inward and laying the paper on the table. The review resolves to a logged outcome: cleared, flagged, identity burned, or dismissed. It's the paranoia of the org made procedural, and it's the natural endgame of a bot that records everything — eventually it gets pointed at you.

### 18. Commendations & Write-ups
Standing needs a paper record. Superiors issue **commendations** (a clean op, a big supply run, a solved problem) and **write-ups** (missed musters, forbidden movements, sloppy paperwork), each logged permanently to the member's file and reflected on the site. It's the formal ledger of reputation that feeds everything else — promotions weigh the file, IA reads the file, a stack of write-ups is grounds for a burn or a dismissal. Small, but it makes the hierarchy feel earned rather than assigned: your record follows you, in writing, forever.

### 19. Financial Reporting & Reconciliation
A reporting layer over the money — not a laundering game, just the due-diligence view a careful company would actually run. On demand or on a schedule, the bot produces a clean financial summary for High Command: white vs black balances, inflows against outflows, requisitions against orders, and — the important one — anything that doesn't reconcile: an inflow without a matching order, an outflow without a signature. It's the "are the books explainable?" report, the thing you'd want in hand before an investigation, and it publishes a public-safe version to the website ledger so the front stays convincing. The tension is entirely diegetic: the numbers add up or they don't, and High Command decides what to do about the gap.

### 20. The Leadership Audit Trail
The company watches everyone — including the people at the top. Every consequential action taken through the bot is written to an immutable trail: who created or burned which identity, who issued which directive, who approved which requisition, who moved which funds, who ran `/lookup` on whom. High Command can act, but they can't act *unseen*, and that record is itself gated at the very highest clearance. It's the most on-brand idea of the set — the logical end of "what is written down is written carefully" — and it quietly answers the question every powerful org eventually faces: who audits the auditors? The bot does. The bot always did.

## Note on the generated IDs
They use the **real format** (standard `XXX-XX-XXXX` SSNs, proper-looking ID cards) so they read right in-fiction — they're just randomly generated numbers attached to made-up characters, never mapped to a real person. Random number in a real format ≠ anyone's actual ID; it's set dressing for fictional characters.
